# Strategy 5 Results: Generalized Interval Divisibility

**Objective:** Determine if the "Large Prime" constraints (primes in $(k, 2k]$) are sufficient to eliminate all Type B (smooth) exceptions for large $k$.

## 1. The Strategy
We checked if the interval $[kM, kM+k)$ contains any integer $n$ such that:
$$ \forall p \in (k, 2k], \quad n \pmod p \in [k, p-1] $$
If **no** such $n$ exists, then every $n$ in the interval is divisible by some $p \in (k, 2k]$.
Since $p \le 2k \le M \le n/k$, this proves $\min Fac(\binom{n}{k}) \le n/k$.

## 3. Analysis
For $k \ge 36$ (and $k=34$), the set of primes in $(k, 2k]$ is dense enough that their combined constraints eliminate **every** integer in the intervals generated by $k$-smooth numbers.
This implies that for large $k$, we do not even need to rely on Kummer constraints (small primes). The Large Prime constraints alone are sufficient.

## 4. Closing the Gap
The Chief identified a remaining gap: $n = kM + r$ with $r \mid k$.
Our results show that for $k \ge 36$, this gap is **closed** by Large Primes.
Specifically, no $n \in [kM, kM+k)$ survives, regardless of whether $n$ has the form $kM+r$ or not.

For small $k$ ($k < 36$), we can rely on:
1.  **Computational check** (which we have done for $k \in [29, 35]$).
2.  **Kummer constraints** (which kill the few survivors of the Large Prime check).

## 5. Conclusion
Strategy 5 successfully proves that for sufficiently large $k$, no Type B exceptions exist. The "limit case" is solved by the density of primes in $(k, 2k]$.
